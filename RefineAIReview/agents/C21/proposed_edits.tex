% NEW:
The long-run player chooses $a_1 \in \{A(\text{cquiesce}), F(\text{ight})\}$. The short-run player, observing the history of $a_1$ but not $\theta$, chooses $a_2 \in \{C(\text{ooperate}), D(\text{efect})\}$. Following Luo \& Wolitzky (2024, Section~2.1), the LR payoff matrix with cooperation gain $g > 0$ and defection loss $l > 0$ is:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
\toprule
& \multicolumn{2}{c}{$a_2 = C$} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{$a_2 = D$} \\
\cmidrule(lr){2-3} \cmidrule(lr){4-5}
$(\theta, a_1)$ & $u_1$ & & $u_1$ & \\
\midrule
$(G, A)$ & $1 + g$ & & $1$ & \\
$(G, F)$ & $x + g$ & & $x$ & \\
$(B, A)$ & $y + g$ & & $y$ & \\
$(B, F)$ & $g$ & & $0$ & \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent with $x, y \in (0,1)$ and $g, l > 0$. The Stackelberg strategy $s_1^*(G) = A$, $s_1^*(B) = F$ maximizes the \emph{reputation payoff}---the expected payoff when SR best-responds to the commitment type---not the per-period payoff against defection. Note that against $D$ alone, $A$ dominates $F$ in state $B$ ($y > 0$); the optimality of $s_1^*(B) = F$ depends on the cooperation column, where it makes the commitment type identifiable and captures the surplus $g$ in good states.
